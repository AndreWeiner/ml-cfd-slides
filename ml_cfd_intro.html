    Example: physics-informed polynomials

    • initial value problem $$ \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t} = -kx,\ x(t=0)=1,\ t\in \left[0,1\right] $$
    • solution $$ x(t) = \mathrm{exp}(-kt) $$
    $$ x(t) = \mathrm{exp}(-kt) $$

    idea: fit polynomial ansatz

    $$ \hat{x}(t) = at^2 + bt + 1 $$
    • using classical regression
    • "physics-informed" regression

    Approach 1: training based on pairs $(t_i, x_i)$

    $$ L(a,b) = \frac{1}{N_p} \sum\limits_{i=1}^{N_p} \left( x_i - \hat{x}(t_i) \right)^2 $$

    solve minimization problem

    $$ \underset{a,b}{\mathrm{argmin}} L(a, b) $$

    Polynomial fit based on data $(x_i, t_i)$.

    Approach 2: training based on ODE evaluated at control points $t_i$

    $$ L(a,b) = \frac{1}{N_c} \sum\limits_{i=1}^{N_c} \left( \left. \frac{\mathrm{d} \hat{x}}{\mathrm{d}t }\right\vert_{t_i} + k\hat{x}(t_i) \right)^2 $$

    solve minimization problem

    $$ \underset{a,b}{\mathrm{argmin}} L(a, b) $$

    Polynomial fit based on ODE evaluated at control points $t_i$.

    Some problems with PINNs:

    • limited number of activation functions
    • optimization is often hard and slow
    • additional tuning parameters
    • governing equations + IC/BC must be known
    • data-driven approximation often faster and more accurate

    active research area $\rightarrow$ might change in the future
